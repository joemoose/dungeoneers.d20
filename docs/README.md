# Preface

Welcome to Dungeoneers! This guide is for players—it’s the resource I hand to new players at my table. It contains all the information you need to create your character and equip them, lock swords with enemies, cast spells, and head out on your adventures.

If you’re new to tabletop roleplaying games, I’d suggest you stop now and first read [*Fundamentals of Tabletop Roleplaying*](https://levikornelsen.itch.io/fundamentals-of-tabletop-roleplaying) by Levi Kornelsen. Kornelsen provides a clear introduction to all aspects of this type of game and his book is a good resource for anyone new to the hobby.

So, for those familair with RPGs, what is Dungeoneers?
Dungeoneers grew out of a desire for a specific style of play. Call it "old school." I wanted a game that, first and foremost, encourages *player* ingenuity over *character* abilities, a game that weaves an emergent story from conversations between the players and GM. To do something, you just need to describe it. The rules disappear and stay well out of the way until needed—perhaps to decide an uncertain or risky outcome. The GM will let you know if and when you need to roll dice. Description, observation, and resourcefulness are the player's tools, not the game’s rules.

As such, Dungeoneers does not use traditional character classes. The equipment your character carries and your creativity and inventiveness as a player define your character's abilities. You won't "use" your character sheet to look up a specific power for a particular problem. If you want to be a warrior, find the best armor and weapons you can afford. A thief needs tools. A mage needs spells. Do all three? Certainly.

Additionally, combat is also highly consequential. Every foe is deadly and can defeat you in a few rounds. There's no back-and-forth slog of dice rolling, standing toe-to-toe, and trading a long series of blows that might or might not hit. Attacks always hit; you just roll damage. There's no "encounter balance." Combat is a puzzle to solve rather than a numbers-based simulation of attrition. You must gain tactical advantages and defeat opponents quickly and overwhelmingly or else retreat and try a different approach.

Given that equipment is essential to a character's abilities, using up your resources during an expedition can be meaningful to a good narrative. But I don't like bookkeeping, so inventory is a simple mechanic: 12 slots maximum for gear and mark usage on consumables. That's it. The GM will let you know when a torch runs out.

This game also has a luck system to encourage a little bit of crazy risk-taking. You are a heroic adventurer, after all.

Enjoy!

Zachary Taylor
