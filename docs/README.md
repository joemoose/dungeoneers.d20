DUNGEONEERS RPG is a rules-light system for playing old-school tabletop fantasy roleplaying adventures.

# Preface

Welcome to Dungeoneers! This guide is for players—it’s the resource I hand to new players at my table. It contains all the information you need to create your character and equip them, lock swords with enemies, cast spells, and head out on your adventures. A player's guide doesn't tell you how to run the world, build interesting challenges or weird places to explore—that's the GM's side of the table and a different guide.

Dungeoneers grew out of a desire for a specific style of play. Call it "old school." I wanted a game that, first and foremost, encourages *player* ingenuity over *character* abilities, a game that weaves an emergent story from conversations between the players and GM. To do something, you just need to describe it. The rules disappear and stay well out of the way until needed—perhaps to decide an uncertain or risky outcome to decide. The GM will let you know if and when you need to roll dice. Description, observation, and resourcefulness are the player's tools, not the game rules.

As such, Dungeoneers is classless. The equipment your character carries and your creativity and inventiveness define your character's abilities. You won't "use" your character sheet to look up a specific power for a particular problem. If you want to be a warrior, find the best armor and weapons you can afford. A thief needs tools. A mage needs spells. Do all three? Certainly.

Additionally, combat is also highly consequential. Every foe is deadly and can defeat you in a few rounds. There's no back-and-forth slog of dice rolling, standing toe-to-toe, and trading a long series of blows that might or might not hit. Attacks always hit; you just roll damage. There's no "encounter balance." Combat is a puzzle to solve rather than a numbers-based simulation of attrition. You must gain tactical advantages and defeat opponents quickly and overwhelmingly or else retreat and try a different approach.

Given that equipment is essential to a character's abilities, using resources an expedition impacts the narrative. But I don't like bookkeeping, so inventory is a simple mechanic: 12 slots maximum for gear and mark usage on consumables. That's it.

This game also has a luck system to encourage a little bit of crazy risk-taking. You are a heroic adventurer, after all.

Enjoy!

Zachary Taylor
