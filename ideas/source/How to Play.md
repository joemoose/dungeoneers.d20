# How to Play

The core of this game is a conversation between the players and the GM. The GM describes the players' current situation. Then the players ask questions and tell the GM what their characters do. The GM explains the results of their actions, and then the players react again and describe more activities, and so on.

The GM answers your questions and always respects your freedom of choice. The GM certainly provides challenges, obstacles, and situations to solve and overcome, but the game is not simply a story narrated by the GM. Instead, it’s an interactive fiction built on your and your fellow players’ decisions. Consider it as both creating and acting in an adventure story simultaneously.

If the outcome of your character’s actions is not immediately apparent, or the activity is inherently risky, the GM might ask you to roll dice to determine the results.

## Actions

When your character attempts something, you say what they want to achieve and how they do it. The GM then responds if the action is **Easy**, **Risky**, or **Impossible**, as well as the expected outcome.

- **Easy**: Something routine or without risk or opposition that would be impossible to fail. The outcome happens as you describe.
- **Risky**: There is opposition, or the outcome is uncertain, and failure has consequences; make an Ability Check.
- **Impossible**: Something impracticable. No dice rolls; try a new approach, or suffer the consequences.

## Ability Checks

For a Risky action, the GM will ask you to make an Ability Check using your character’s Ability Score—BRAWN, BRAINS, or LUCK—most applicable to the situation.

- **Roll d20**. You succeed if you roll **equal to** or **less than** the Ability Score. You suffer no consequences.

### Critical Successes and Fumbles

When you roll your **exact** score on an Ability Check, this is called a Critical Success. The action always succeeds, and the outcome is better than you expected. **Permanently increase** your LUCK Score by 1.

However, when you roll a **natural 20**, you’ve had a Critical Failure, also called a Fumble. You suffer the consequences of failure, and the outcome is worse than expected. **Permanently decrease** your LUCK Score by 1.

### Advantage and Disadvantage

Suppose your character has a situational **advantage** on their Ability Check. If so, you roll an **extra d20** (2d20) and keep the **best** single result. If they are at a disadvantage, roll two d20 and use the **worst** single result.

### Skills

Skills represent areas of expertise for your characters, such as Acrobatics or Trading. Skills grant an advantage on relevant ability checks. The GM might even rule that an otherwise risky action succeeds automatically if you have a relevant skill and proper equipment (for example, you have the “Healing” skill and are using a healer’s toolkit to treat a battle wound).

## Time and Distance

Time and distance are abstracted and flexible, depending on the current narrative. 

### Measuring Time

The game measures time in three durations: round, turn, and watch.

- **Round** is the time scale for combat. Each Round is less than a minute.
- **Turn** is the time scale for exploration. Each Turn is around 10 minutes, enough time to explore a room and perform an Action or two.
- **Watch** is the time scale used when traveling. Each Watch is 6 hours: morning, afternoon, evening, and night.

### Measuring Distance

The game uses **zones** as abstractions of distance to divide larger physical spaces into smaller units, like rooms in a building.

Zones could be areas of a castle courtyard, parts of a colossal beast, or contiguous city districts.

For example, at the start of combat, the GM divides the battlefield into zones. The number of zones can vary, but generally, a battlefield is made up of at least three zones; one where the party starts, one where the enemies start, and the area between them.

Creatures in the same zone are at close range and can physically interact with each other (melee attacks, exchange items, talk to each other, etc.).

The number of zones can change throughout combat. For example, suppose a creature uses a magical power to levitate. In that case, it creates a new flying zone, which creatures without the ability to fly can’t enter (but ranged weapons could shoot into).

The exact distance within a zone is generally not of importance. A reference:

- **Close**: Within the same zone. You can touch it. Most actions affect things in your zone. 
- **Near**: 1 zone away. You can get to it.
- **Far**: 2 zones away. You can shoot at it. 
- **Distant**: 3 zones away. You can see it.

## How to use Luck

LUCK is a unique Attribute; you can permanently decrease (burn) it to modify a BRAWN or BRAINS save into your favor, adding or removing the amount spent from the roll result. Players can’t benefit from Critical Success this way.

The GM may call for a LUCK check for truly random events.

LUCK is also volatile and can be modified with the character’s interactions in the game. Besides critical successes or fumbles, LUCK can also change in these ways:

- **Favor or Disfavor of a Powerful Being**: Destroy a God’s temple, do a favor for an Archdemon, insult a Fey Lord, etc. Such powerful entities can sway the winds of fate to your benefit or detriment if you give them a reason to.
- **Achieve an Important Goal**: Every adventurer wishes to achieve great things: build a fortress, slay a dragon, ascend to godhood, amass a fortune, etc. When you finally reach your goal or take a significant step towards it, your LUCK increases.

The GM might change a character’s LUCK because of other impactful events. No matter the reason, a change in LUCK is always a +1/-1 modifier at most.
