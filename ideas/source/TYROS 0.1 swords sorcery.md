# Introduction

TYROS is an old-school fantasy roleplaying adventure game for one facilitator (the Game Master or GM) and at least one other player.

Players assume the roles of adventures exploring an ancient world filled with strange folk, treasure, foul sorcery, and horrific monstrosities.

## What This Is

- A role-playing game (RPG) is a conversational game where everyone at the table plays in a shared world of imagination.
- This game centers on questions, creativity, and nuance, forming an emergent story.
- Player characters are empowered but vulnerable. The world is vast and filled with places to explore.

## Who You Are

- The GM is a neutral arbiter of the world, presenting information and circumstances.
- The players take the world at face value, treating it as real, consistent, and full of risk and reward.
- Together, you trust each other to engage with a shared setting, objectives, and consequences.

## Sword & Sorcery

- The universe is decadent, mostly dead, and without interest in cosmic good or redemption.
- Any given world is rent apart by ancient machinery, fell sorceries, and intervening godlings.
- Humankind bands together to manage a living, but tensions and threats are everywhere.

## Picaresque Adventure

- You play as and compete with daring scoundrels out for treasure, power, and reputation.
- Opportunities abound for high-stakes expeditions. Your context is dangerous, but you have grit.
- Fair fights are bad odds. You must negotiate, plot, haggle, ally, and deceive to survive.

## Inspiration

- Howard's *Cimmeria*, where Conan slogged against petty rulers and snake people for profit and renown.
- Vance's *Dying Earth*, where Cugel crossed through portals to far sanctums while his world rotted.
- Lieber's *Lankhmar*, where Fafhrd and the Gray Mouser fleeced nobles with bounties on their heads.

# Creating a Character

You are a newly minted adventurer: a tomb-raiding, treasure-seeking ne’er-do-well who wields a spellbook just as easily as a blade. You will live a dangerous life. You will delve into strange and mysterious places. With some luck, you might retire before dying.

## 1. Roll Attribute Scores

Three attributes describe your character’s primary characteristics:

- ***BODY***: strength, constitution, and dexterity
- ***MIND***: intelligence, wisdom, and charisma
- ***LUCK***: good fortune, favor of the gods

Are they strong or insightful? Or a little of both? Unlucky at cards or a darling of Fortuna?

➡️  Roll **2d6+4** three times, noting the results. Assign the lowest value to your LUCK and the other two as you prefer between BODY and MIND.

## 2. Roll Hit Points

Your Hit Points (HP) are the amount of damage you can shrug off before suffering an injury. HP is easily and quickly restored; an injury is more serious.

➡️  Roll 2d4 to determine your starting hit points.

## 3: Choose a Background

Now let’s find out a little backstory on your character.

➡️  Choose or randomly select a background in the following Character Backgrounds section. 

## 4. Choose an Archetype

What type of adventurer would you like to play? A warrior, a thief, or a sorcerer?

➡️  Pick an archetype for your character from the following Character Archetypes section. The archetype grants your character special features, which you should note on your character sheet. For example, the warrior gains combat maneuvers.

## 5. Starting Equipment

Next, let’s see what equipment you’ve brought to your new adventuring life.

➡️  Everyone starts with a cloak of their choice of color, a dagger (*d4*, *16*), and 2d6 coins.

Choose four more items from the following list. You may choose an item more than once.

- axe (*d8, 10*) or spear (*d6/d8, 14*)
- shortbow (*d6, 14*) and arrows
- gambeson (1 *armor*)
- helmet (+1 *armor*)
- shield (+1 *armor*, -2 *speed*)
- 50 coins
- bandages
- bear trap
- a bottle of fine wine
- fancy clothes (describe)
- fine instrument (describe)
- a flask of fire oil
- healing potion
- hunting horn
- ink, quill, & parchment
- lockpicks
- pipe leaf
- pole, 10ft
- small but vicious dog (name, describe)
- spellbook (*random spell*)

### Group Equipment 

In addition to your individual starting equipment, your party of adventurers begins with the following exploration resources:

- **Illumination** (torches, lanterns, fuel)
- **Climbing gear** (rope, pitons, gloves)
- **Camping gear** (tents, bedrolls, tarps)
- **Rations** (food, water, mess kits)

Distribute each of these resources across all characters as evenly as possible. Each resource is bulky (taking two inventory slots). So long as these resources are not depleted, the party has enough for everyone.

## 6. Choose a Name and Appearance

➡️   Give your character a name and think about how they look, their relationship with the other characters in the group, and how their background has influenced them to become the person they are at the start of the game.

You don’t have to decide all this now; developing your character as you play is okay. Be careful not to get too attached; it’s a dangerous world.

If you’d like inspiration or prefer randomly determining your character’s traits, consult the following Character Traits tables.

Good luck, adventurer!

## Character Backgrounds

Your background describes your life before becoming an adventurer and determines your starting skills and languages.

### Cultist

You are privy to obscure mysteries not known to outsiders. Did you break with your brothers and sisters, or do you still travel to further your cult’s tenets?

- **Skills**: Religion, Spirits, Rumors
- **Languages**: Trade Tongue, Vinčan

### Houseborn

Born into a minor Merchant House, you’ve been trained to play markets and people since birth. Are you the last of a dying House or one of many kin with no other way to leave your mark on your House’s legacy?

- **Skill**: Influence, Politics, Trade
- **Languages**: Trade Tongue, Treden script

### Muscle

Whether you were a gladiator, a hired thug, or a prestigious House’s guard, you know how to hold your own in a skirmish. Did you get tired of fighting for a master, or are you just looking for a new challenge?

- **Skills**: Intimidation, Brute Force, Tactics
- **Languages**: Trade Tongue, Argot

### Nomad

You traveled the wilds with your tribe, away from the meddling of the Houses. Did you set out on a task given to you by your tribe’s elders, or were you banished from their tents and left to wander on your own?

- **Skills**: Healing, Survival, Herbalism
- **Languages**: Trade Tongue, an additional language of your choice.

### Performer

You were part of a traveling troupe of actors, artists, and acrobats, showing off your skills to cheering crowds. Are you searching for inspiration for your next act or an audience more appreciative of your talents?

- **Skills**: Art, Performance, Acrobatics
- **Languages**: Trade Tongue

### Scavenger

You are familiar with the dangers lurking in the shattered lands and how to get your hands on the hidden relics. Are you in it for the gold or to sate your curiosity?

- **Skills**: Exploring, Stealth, Traps
- **Languages**: Trade Tongue, Argot

### Scribe

You spent years of your life inscribing and illuminating other people’s tales in dry tomes. Did you leave the scriptorium of your own accord, or were you forced to quit?

- **Skills**: History, Culture, Geography
- **Languages**: Trade Tongue, Treden script

### Worker

A life of toiling in iron mines, working the fields, or building the strongholds and halls of House nobles has hardened your body and spirit. Did you take pride in your work, or are you happy to have left it behind?

- **Skills**: Endurance, Animals, Crafting 
- **Languages**: Trade Tongue

## Character Archetypes

There are three basic archetypes: warrior, thief, and sorcerer. These archetypes add unique talents to your character but are only a starting point.

A single class does not limit your character’s role or abilities. The equipment they carry and their experiences define their specialty. Attributes, skills, and related saves do not determine your character; they are tools.

Look not to your character sheet for answers! Instead, be creative with your intuition, items, and connections. Asking questions and listening to details is more valuable than any stat.

### WARRIOR

Fighters are adventurers dedicated to mastering the arts of combat and war. In a group of adventurers, the role of fighters is to battle monsters and defend other characters.

#### Talent: Combat Maneuvers

A warrior has mastered a variety of special maneuvers you can perform in combat. You do this by spending a resource called **grit**. You start with one grit. At every **even-numbered** level, you gain one additional grit. You may use multiple maneuvers at the same time.

The grit you spend is restored after you take a long rest.

##### Level 1 Maneuvers

A new warrior has three maneuvers in their repertoire:

- **FOCUS**: When making an attack, you may spend one grit to ignore the target’s Armor.
- **NOT ON MY WATCH!**: You may spend one grit to re-roll your Speed roll when determining combat order.
- **SHAKE IT OFF**: Whenever you take damage, you may spend one grit to reduce the damage by 1d6.

##### Level 2, 5, & 7 Maneuvers

Choose one of the following maneuvers at 2nd, 5th, and 7th levels and add it to your repertoire.

- **COMMAND**: Instead of making an attack, you may spend one grit to allow an ally to make an attack that deals +1d6 additional damage.
- **PAYBACK**: When an ally takes damage from an enemy within the range of your weapon, you may spend one grit to attack them immediately.
- **SHIELD**: When an ally you share a zone with takes damage, you may spend a grit and add your AR to theirs for this Round (this can increase it above 6).
- **SPLIT FIRE**: When you use a ranged weapon, you may spend one grit to shoot at two separate targets.
- **TAUNT**: You may spend one grit to make all enemies attack you this Round.
- **RALLY**: At the start of a Round, spend one grit to give you and your allies +1 additional damage until the end of the current Round.
- **CLEAVE**: Instead of making a normal attack, you may spend one grit to make a single attack hitting up to four enemies in your zone. This attack deals half damage but still ignores Armor on a critical hit.

#### Talent: Trophies

You gain the following warrior abilities at 3rd level.

**TROPHIES**

After you defeat a creature in combat, you may take a trophy from their remains. A trophy takes up one item slot.

When you encounter a creature of which you carry a trophy, you may spend one grit to:

- Receive +d6 bonus damage when attacking it.
- Gain an advantage on a save against that creature’s abilities.

**SPOOR**

You can also construct a trophy by scouting a creature’s lair and taking something from it or spending at least a week researching the creature using adequate resources. This spoor trophy is destroyed after it is used once.

### THIEF

Thieves are adventurers who live by their wits, with a little resourcefulness and finesse for good measure. Their range of unique talents makes them versatile companions in adventures.

#### Talents

You have a variety of unique abilities. When you create your character, and each time you gain a level, you may choose one talent from the following list or gain mastery of a talent you already know.

**BACKSTAB**: When you attack a creature that isn’t aware of you, in addition to the advantage you would typically gain, you double the damage rolled.

- **Mastery**: During combat, as part of your attack, you may make a BODY or MIND save against a creature aware of you to flank or confuse the target, adding a +d12 damage bonus.

**EXPERTISE**: You may re-roll an ability save once per session.

- **Mastery**: You may re-roll any dice roll.

**MANY POCKETS**: It no longer takes an action to retrieve an item from your backpack slots during combat.

- **Mastery**: You gain two additional backpack slots.

**NETWORK**: Make a MIND save when entering a new settlement. If you succeed, note that you have a contact in the settlement. The nature of this contact is up to you and the GM and can be fleshed out whenever the need arises.

- **Mastery**: You may make a MIND save once each session to see if you have a contact in an area, regardless of whether you’re new to the area.

**SUPPLIES**: You may buy a Supply for 10 coins in a settlement. A Supply is an abstract item with a single use available only to the thief that takes up one inventory slot. It can be changed into any *common or cheap* mundane item.

- **Mastery**: A supply can also produce a *rare or valuable* mundane item.

**SWIFT**: You no longer trigger a free attack when disengaging.

- **Mastery**: You always act before the enemy if you’re not surprised and not wearing heavy armor or wielding a shield.

### SORCERER

Anyone can cast magic from spellbooks, scrolls, and relics. Sorcerers, though, possess an innate magical power. Whether it arises from an exotic bloodline, otherworldly influence, or exposure to unknown corrupting forces, no one chooses sorcery; the power chooses the sorcerer.

A sorcerer brings substantial capabilities to an adventuring party, but a sorcerer risks losing their souls, if not their lives, to the fell corruptions of their art. 

#### Talents

**Sorcereous Powers**: You alter the world through sheer force of will and your body’s consumption. You need no charms, runes, spellbooks, or scrolls. Reality is yours to command.

Your sorcerous power requires you to expend at least one point of BODY, MIND, or LUCK as essence to fuel your magic. The more of your body you sacrifice, the greater the essence consumed and the greater the magnitude of the power unleashed.

Ability scores lost in invoking your powers are healed as usual—BODY and MIND from long rests, LUCK from critical success rolls, and so on. When losing BODY attribute scores for essence, a critical damage save is not required. 

**To invoke a sorcerous power**, sacrifice abilities scores for essence and make a MIND save. If the save succeeds, the power manifests as you intended. The power does not materialize on a failure; you must make a corruption save to avoid the sorcerous backlash affecting you.

The invocation has an advantage if performed over two rounds without interruption. The GM may allow the invocation to succeed automatically when invoking your power uninterrupted as a ritual over a watch. 

#### Powers

When you create your character, pick one power. You may select an additional power at 3rd, 5th, and 7th levels.

##### ALTER

Make a declarative statement affecting one creature or object you can see. The statement is true for [essence] combat rounds. The creation cannot cause damage directly (use Harm), move a creature or object, or create new things or effects (use Create). Sentient creatures and magical objects can save to avoid being altered.

The maximum size or weight of the creature or object to be altered is that of the sorcerer. You can double the size or weight altered for each additional essence. 

> "This door does not exist." You can walk through the space the door formerly occupied.  
> "The giant is now a mole.” The giant retains its abilities and HP, but it would be easier to surround a mole and attack.  
> "The dragon is made of paper." The dragon is now made of paper. It retains its abilities and HP, but it will take extra damage if you set it on fire with a torch.  

##### CREATE

Bring forth a creation. The creature or object created exists for [essence] rounds. The creation is, at most, the size or weight of the sorcerer. For each additional essence, you can double the size or weight of your creation. 

More essence can be sacrificed to imbue the creation with specific properties or powers beyond its basic form. Creations cannot deal damage directly (use Harm), nor are they sentient. You can create objects with magical effects (flying carpets, invisibility cloaks), but these objects do not deal magic damage or provide permanent results. For example, HP restored from an invoked healing potion lasts only for the duration of your invocation.

> Zathar the Sorcerer invokes a flying throne, which he mounts to escape. He sacrifices six essences (the throne is *2 times his size* and can *fly* for *3 rounds*).

##### HARM

Deal an [essence] number of d6 damage to one target creature or object you can see. Creatures and magical objects can make a save to avoid or mitigate the damage. Describe your manifestations however you’d like: electrical arcs, exudations of fire from your fingertips, grasping hands from the underworld. Sacrifice an essence for each additional target affected.

> Zathar sacrifices two essences. He can Harm one creature for 2d6 damage or two creatures for 1d6 damage each.

##### SOUL SIGHT

You can see an [essence] number of creatures’ souls as long as you concentrate exclusively on the observation and do not move. This allows you to sense the approximate location of invisible creatures. You can also immediately sense if a person is possessed, undead, protected by the gods, lying, a spellcaster, or in other metaphysiological states.

##### WARD

Manifest a simmering, translucent shield around you or a target creature or object for [essence x2] rounds. The ward provides an advantage on saves to avoid magical effects and a disadvantage on damage from physical attacks. The ward moves with its target. The ward can be sundered and destroyed to negate all damage from a single spell or attack.

#### Dark Blood Magic

The essence required for sorcerous powers can be drained from another sentient creature. Doing so invites abomination and chaos into the sorcerer and increases their corruption immediately by 1. A corruption save is required regardless of whether the invocation succeeds.

The process is not as efficient as draining essence from the sorcerer’s body; two points of BODY or MIND must be sacrificed from the host for one essence. LUCK cannot be drained from a host. The essence drained from the host can be combined with that from the sorcerer.

If essence is drained from an unwilling victim, the victim must be restrained, and, assuming they are conscious, they can make a save to resist the loss of ability scores.

# The Basics

The core of this game is a conversation between the players and the GM. The GM describes the players' current situation. Then the players ask questions and tell the GM what their characters do. The GM explains the results of their actions, and then the players react again and describe more activities, and so on.

The GM answers your questions and always respects your freedom of choice. The GM certainly provides challenges, obstacles, and situations to solve and overcome, but the game is not simply a story narrated by the GM. Instead, it’s an interactive fiction built on your and your fellow players’ decisions. Consider it as both creating and acting in an adventure story simultaneously.

If the outcome of your character’s actions is not immediately apparent, or the activity is inherently risky, the GM might ask you to roll dice to determine the results.

## Attributes

Three attributes describe your character’s primary characteristics:

- ***BODY***: strength, constitution, and dexterity
- ***MIND***: intelligence, wisdom, and charisma
- ***LUCK***: good fortune, favor of the gods

Whenever your character attempts an action, one of these attributes will apply when resolving the outcome. For example, pushing open a stuck door, resisting the effects of poison, or balancing on a narrow beam involves your BODY attribute. Understanding an ancient tome, searching for clues, or bluffing your way past a guard involves MIND. Something random, like who does the falling rock land on, is your LUCK.

## Actions

When your character attempts something, you say what they want to achieve and how they do it. The GM then responds if the action is **Easy**, **Risky**, or **Impossible**, as well as the expected outcome.

- **Easy**: Something routine or without risk or opposition that would be impossible to fail. The outcome happens as you describe.
- **Risky**: There is opposition, or the outcome is uncertain, and failure has consequences; make a Save.
- **Impossible**: Something impracticable. No dice rolls; try a new approach, or suffer the consequences.

## Saves

A Save determines the outcome of a risky action or situation. Roll d20. You succeed if your roll is equal to or less than the applicable attribute score. Rolling the exact attribute score is a critical success, and rolling a 20 is a critical failure.

## Advantage and Disadvantage

If your character has a situational advantage on a save, roll two d20s and keep the best result. If they are at a disadvantage, roll two d20s and keep the worst result.

## Skills

Skills are the areas of expertise of your character that grant an advantage on relevant saves. The GM may rule that a save for a risky action succeeds automatically if you have a suitable talent and the appropriate equipment. 

## Luck

On BODY and MIND saves with a critical success, increase your LUCK score permanently by 1. On those saves with a critical failure, decrease your LUCK score by 1.

You can permanently decrease your LUCK anytime to modify BODY and MIND saves, adding or removing the amount spent from the roll result.

LUCK may also be changed per the GM by impactful in-game events, such as achieving important goals or garnering a powerful being’s favor or enmity.

The GM may sometimes call for LUCK save if an outcome is random and BODY or MIND attributes aren’t relevant.

## Time

The game measures time in three durations:

- **Round** is the time scale for combat. Each Round is around 10 seconds.
- **Turn** is the time scale for exploration. Each Turn is around 10 minutes, enough time to explore a room and perform an Action or two.
- **Watch** is the time scale used when traveling. Each Watch is 6 hours: morning, afternoon, evening, and night.

## Distances and Zones

The game uses **zones** as abstractions of distance and area to divide larger physical spaces into smaller units, like rooms in a house.

For example, at the start of combat, the GM divides the battlefield into at least three zones; one where the party starts, one where the enemies start, and the area between them.

The number of zones can change throughout combat. For example, suppose a creature uses a magical power to levitate. In that case, it creates a new flying zone, which creatures without the ability to fly can’t enter (but ranged weapons could shoot into).

Conditions within zones may also vary. For instance, a zone could represent a narrow bridge that contains only a limited number of combatants. Another zone could be littered with boulders providing excellent cover against ranged attacks. A zone could also be a hilltop, thus representing higher ground that provides an advantage on attacks. Conditions could also change during a battle. A dragon’s fiery breath might set a wooded zone ablaze.

The distance within and between zones is relative:

- **Close**: Within the same zone. You can touch it. Most actions affect things in your zone. 
- **Near**: 1 zone away. You can get to it.
- **Far**: 2 zones away. You can shoot at it. 
- **Distant**: 3 zones away. You can see it.

## Character Advancement

As your character adventures and explores more of their world, they’ll improve their capabilities through events in the narrative. They’ll potentially toughen up their HP and stats through close brushes with death, discover new magical relics and spellbooks, and spend hard-earned treasures and coins to upgrade and expand their equipment.

To learn new skills, they’ll find trainers and patrons. There are opportunities for growth and change everywhere if players seek them out.

### Experience Points

You will also receive experience points (XP) you can spend on improvement.

There are two sources of XP: 

- Each time you fail a BODY or MIND save, mark 1 XP. You learn from failure more than from success.

- At the end of each session, the GM and players will answer three questions as a group. 

	- Did we learn something new and important about the world?
	- Did we overcome a notable monster or enemy?
	- Did we loot a memorable treasure?

For each “yes,” everyone receives a point of XP.

### Gaining a Level

When your player has accumulated XP equal to their current level plus seven, they may spend the XP to gain a level. For example, to level from 1st to 2nd requires 8 XP, and from 2nd to 3rd requires 9.

After leveling up, do the following:

- Roll 3d6 for each attribute—BODY, MIND, and LUCK. If a result is higher than its current maximum score, increase that score by 1. If none of your stats increase after rolling all three, increase the lowest score by 1.
- Roll 3d6 for HP. If the result is higher than your current maximum HP, increase it by 1.
- Check your archetype to see whether you’ve gained new features. For example, a warrior will add one point of grit at every even-numbered level. 

The maximum score for attributes and HP is 18.

# Combat

Combat is dangerous, and wise adventurers seek to avoid it. Still, sometimes violence is inevitable. 

## Initiative

Before combat commences, the GM determines if a side is surprised. If so, that side does not act in the first round of the battle.

Afterward, players make Initiative checks at the start of each round. Before rolling the dice, each player chooses to attack with a weapon or take another action.

If they choose to attack with a weapon, they declare which weapon they use and roll a d20. If the result is equal to or less than their weapon’s Speed, that character acts before the enemy. If the result is higher, they act after the enemy has acted.

Otherwise, use a default Speed of 10 for other actions.

## Actions

During a round, a character can move from one zone to an adjacent zone and perform a single action. This action can be anything within reason, such as attacking, negotiating, making another move to an adjacent zone, or casting a spell.

### Adhoc Maneuvers

For other possible actions involving an opposing combatant other than attacking, like pushing a combatant into another zone, disarming, knocking them down, or grappling them, the party most at most risk should make a relevant save to see if the action succeeds.

### Backpack Items

Retrieving items from a backpack slot during combat takes an action.

### Disengaging

If you want to move to another zone while an enemy occupies your zone, you must make a successful BODY save. If you fail, the enemy makes a free attack before you move out of the zone.

## Attacking

**Attacks always hit**. Declare a target and roll your weapon’s damage dice. The result is damage to your target’s HP minus its Armor Rating (AR). Remember, this is true for your opponents as well. Their attacks will always hit you.

If attacking with advantage, roll damage twice and deal the best result. If attacking with a disadvantage, roll damage twice and deal the worst result.

### Bonus Damage

Attacks may receive a bonus die, noted as +d6, +d8, and so on. Roll the extra die with your weapon’s damage roll, keeping the best result. Attacks against helpless or unaware targets always receive +d12 Bonus Damage.

### Critical Hits

Rolling the maximum damage on a weapon’s damage dice is a critical hit. The attack ignores the target’s armor and deals the total damage amount.

### Impaired Attacks

Impaired attacks, such as firing through cover or fighting unarmed, roll only d4 damage, and maximum damage does not result in a critical hit. 

### Dual-Wielding

If dual-wielding a set of weapons, use the lowest speed among them to determine initiative, but roll damage for both and choose which result to use.

### Multiple Attackers 

If more than one character attacks the same target, all attackers roll their damage but keep only the highest single result. After the first attacker, an extra point of damage is added to the final result for each additional attacker. 

### Ranged Attacks

Ranged weapons cannot be used if you are in the same zone as the target.

### Aiming

Using an action to aim instead of attacking while not moving allows a character to ignore the target’s armor with a ranged weapon the next Round.

### Blast

Attacks with the *blast* quality affect all targets in the zone, rolling damage separately for each affected combatant. Blast refers to anything from fireballs to massive cleaving onslaughts to a swarm of tiny creatures. If unsure how many targets can be affected in the zone, roll the related damage die for a total number.

### Mounted

Unmounted attacks against mounted targets in the same zone are at a disadvantage. Conversely, mounted attacks against unmounted targets in the same zone have an advantage.

### Subdual

Rather than inflicting grievous injury, you can attempt to subdue an opponent using a melee weapon. Declare that you attempt to subdue and attack normally but halve the damage. An opponent who would suffer a critical injury or death from a subdual attack is instead knocked unconscious.

## Morale

Enemies must pass a MIND save to avoid fleeing when they take their first casualty and again when they lose half their numbers, or their leader is defeated.

Some groups may use their leader’s MIND score instead of their own. Lone foes must save when they’re reduced to 0 HP. Morale does not affect players.

## Warbands

 Large groups of similar opponents fighting together are sometimes treated as one unit. When a warband takes critical damage, it is routed or significantly weakened. When it reaches zero BODY, it is destroyed.

Attacks against a warband by individuals have a disadvantage (excluding blast damage). Attacks against individuals by warbands have an advantage and deal blast damage.

# Harm & Healing

Injury and the risk of death are constant companions of adventurers who explore fantasy gaming worlds.

## Damage

When a character takes damage, they lose that much HP. If they take more damage than their remaining HP, they are injured, and any remaining Damage is removed from their BODY score. They must make a BODY Save using the new score to avoid Critical Damage.

### Armor

Before calculating damage to HP, if a character is wearing armor, subtract the armor rating (AR) from the damage roll. A character can never have more than 6 AR, even if items would increase it above that number.

Wielding a shield increases AR but reduces weapon speed. Wearing a helmet also increases your armor rating.

### Critical Damage

If a character suffers Critical Damage, they cannot do anything but crawl slowly until they are tended to by an ally and have a Short Rest. They’ll die of their injuries if left unattended for an hour.

## Attribute Loss and Death

If a character’s BODY score is reduced to zero, they are dead. If the MIND score is reduced to zero, they fall into a stupor. All dice rolls are disadvantaged if the LUCK score is reduced to zero.

If your PC dies, make a new one, and the GM will introduce them to the party as quickly as possible.

Some unusual spells or monsters may damage the MIND attribute rather than BODY.

## Deprivation and Fatigue

If deprived of a crucial need, such as food and water, your character can no longer recover HP or attributes from resting. For each day deprived, add a Fatigue to your inventory. Fatigue occupies one slot and lasts until your character is no longer deprived and takes a long rest. Casting spells, injury, and other events may also add fatigue.

## Healing

There are three ways for your character to rest and recover:

- **Short Rest**: Resting for a turn restores all lost HP but may expose the party.
- **Long Rest**: Resting for a watch uninterrupted restores 1 point of lost BODY and MIND and removes all Fatigue.
- **Full Rest**: Resting for a week in a safe location fully restores BODY and MIND and removes most long-term injury conditions.

# Equipment

## Inventory Slots

- **Hand Slots**: Items being held, such as weapons and shields.
- **Body Slots**: Items worn for immediate access during combat, like secondary weapons or ammunition for ranged weapons. Armor also takes up a body slot.
- **Backpack Slots**: Items not needed in a hurry. Items in backpack slots take an action to retrieve during combat.

# Magic

Magic items are gleaned from the hoards of conquered monsters or discovered in long-­lost vaults. Such items grant capabilities a character could rarely have otherwise or complement their owner’s capabilities in wondrous ways.

## Spellbooks and Scrolls

Spellbooks contain a single spell and take up one inventory slot. They cannot be transcribed or created; they are recovered from ancient tombs, dungeons, or a wizard’s tower.

Spellbooks sometimes display unusual properties or limitations, such as producing a foul or unearthly smell when opened, possessing an innate intelligence, or being legible only when viewed in moonlight.

Spellbooks will attract the attention of those who seek the arcane power within, and it is considered dangerous to display them openly.

Scrolls are similar to Spellbooks, however:

- They do not take up an inventory slot.
- They do not cause Fatigue.
- They disappear after one use.

## Casting Spells

Anyone can cast a spell from a spellbook by holding it in both hands and reading its contents aloud. They must then add a Fatigue to their inventory, occupying one slot.

If your character is deprived or in danger, the GM may require that you make a MIND save to avoid any corruption from casting the spell.

## Relics

Relics are arcane items imbued with a magical spell or wonderous power. They do not cause Fatigue when used. However, when using relics, you can sometimes risk corruption. Relics usually have limited uses, as well as a recharge condition.

### Attunement

Some relics require your character to bond with them before their magical properties can be used. This bond is called attunement, and certain items have a prerequisite for attuning, such as attuning in a temple or underground.

Attuning to an item requires your character to spend a long rest focused on only that item while being in physical contact with it. This focus can take the form of weapon practice (for a weapon), meditation (for a wondrous object), or some other appropriate activity. At the end of an uninterrupted long rest, your character understands how to activate any magical properties of the relic, including any necessary command words.

A relic can be attuned to only one person at a time, and a person can be attuned to no more than three relics simultaneously. 

Attunement ends if you’ve been more than 100 feet away from the relic for at least a day, if your character dies, or if another creature attunes to the relic. A creature can also voluntarily end attunement unless the relic is cursed.

## Corruption and Fell Sorcery

Magic is volatile and warps its practitioners. Sorcerous corruption is everywhere in the Shattered Lands. 

Your corruption score can be increased by using sorcerous powers, attuning arcane relics, or committing heinous acts. A newly created character has a zero corruption score.

As your Corruption score increases, you suffer increasingly unpleasant effects.

### Corruption Save 

When directed by the GM to make a corruption save, roll d20. If the result is equal to or greater than your current corruption score, you hold chaos at bay (this time) and suffer no consequences.

Otherwise, roll a subsequent d20, add your current corruption score, and consult the Mark of Chaos table for the effect.

Regardless of success or failure, increase your corruption score by 1.

### Transmogrification

After gaining a **fifth** Aspect of the same kind, you are warped into a monster, become an immediate threat to your adventuring party, and a long-term threat to the game world. The GM takes over your character. It’s time to create a new adventurer.

- **Chthonian**: Your character crystallizes into an obsidian pillar of hideous architecture, corrupting the landscape.
- **Demonian**: Your character gains mastery over souls, seeking to ensorcell and enthrall the region’s leaders and beyond.
- **Ophidian**: Your character fully transforms into a horrible serpentine malison, vowing enmity on all who trusted you.
