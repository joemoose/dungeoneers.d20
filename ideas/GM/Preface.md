Preface

Welcome to the GM’s Guide for Dungeonners.

Before we begin, I assume you are already familiar with the fundamentals of game mastering and have run at least a session or two.

If you’re new at this, I’ll recommend several resources so that you can dig a little deeper into our hobby and try your hand at game mastering. Running a game is an enjoyable experience, perhaps even more so than participating as a player at the table.

A GM’s character is the entire world around the player characters, its weird occupants, and curious, mysterious places.

==add references to “how to GM” books==


[The Practical Guide to Becoming a Great Game Master](https://www.greatgamemaster.com/dm/product/the-practical-guide-to-becoming-a-great-gm-digital/) by Guy Sclanders.


A GM takes on many rewarding responsibilities--subject matter expert, impartial arbiter of rules and rulings, enthusiastic champion of the player characters, improvisational actor, motivator, collector of books, writer, enthusiast of the odd, and, of course, weaver of stories. It’s a craft worth pursuing.

If you have experience as a game master, this guide will offer advice and examples on how to run an “old school” game. As described in the player’s guide, my goal with Dungeoneers is a system that encourages player *ingenuity* over character *abilities*. A system that develops its narrative through a conversation between you and the players. You might need a few new arrows in your GM quiver to facilitate this type of adventure game.

Enjoy!

Zachary Taylor

Old School

Your role as GM in an old-school game differs from that in a traditional RPG. Your job isn’t to remember and apply rules correctly; it’s to make up on-the-spot rulings and colorfully describe them. It’s your job to answer questions and provide the players with many decisions. You are the rulebook. Just as the players need to lose the idea that their characters are adventuring in a balanced, pre-plotted, structured environment, you must abandon the idea that a set of rules and mechanics govern situations. Rules do not govern situations; you govern situations. Focus on making the situations fun, not “properly” run.
