# Complications

Adapted from *[Danger Cards](https://www.drivethrurpg.com/product/152383/Danger-Cards)* by Levi Kornelsen; used with permission.

## Introduction

When a character describes an action that is dangerous with an uncertain outcome, you describe the possible complications that could arise. Your complications increase the stakes and add interesting twists to the narrative. The character may rethink their approach, and that’s certainly okay, even encouraged, if their action is fraught with disadvantages. Their new descriptions may also change the number or content of your complications.

## Assigning Complications

Assign **two to four** complications for any action requiring a dice roll. The more dramatic, epic, or just plain dangerous the situation, the more complications you should add.

Two complications can often be avoided, while four complications generally result in at least one repercussion the party will have to deal with.

### Doubling Up

A complication can be assigned more than once. Typing to trip an elephant presents multiple opportunities to be trampled.

### Constraints

Many complications, such as fear and injuries, could add a *constraint* to a player’s inventory.

## Avoiding Complications

After the character rolls their attribute check, the closer they roll to their attribute score, without going over, the more of your complications they avoid. This approach is called the “**roll under, high**” dice mechanic.

In any outcome, the **player** chooses which complications are avoided.

- If the player **rolls their attribute score exactly**, a critical success, they **avoid all** the complications, regardless of how many you put in front of them.

- They can avoid **three** complications if they successfully roll **within 2** of their score. Any other successful roll avoids **two** complications.

- Even if they **fail** their check, they can still avoid **one** of the complications. However, if they **roll a 20**, a critical failure, they **suffer all** the complications.

!!! note “Example”

	For example, let’s assume a player is making a check against a 14 attribute score, and you’ve presented four complications to make their life interesting.
	- If they roll a 14, they avoid all four complications. Huzzah!
	- They can avoid three complications if they roll a 12 or a 13.
	- Rolling 11 or less removes two of the four.
	- Conversely, if they fail the roll with a 15 or higher, they remove only one complication and suffer the remaining three.
	- Of course, if they roll a 20, they suffer all four. Zounds!

## Complications

The following table lists and describes possible complications you can use in describing possible outcomes for your player’s actions. The list starts with the most basic complication—failure (they don’t achieve their result)—and increasingly layers on more danger or harmful consequences. The descriptions are written from the point of view of the player.

###  Basic 

| Complication | Description |
| :-- | :-- | 
| Failure | You don’t achieve what you were attempting. |
| Forfeit | You miss an opportunity or fail to notice something besides your main goal. |
| Delay | You take more time than you expected. |
| Expense | You use up resources, such as ammunition, supplies, materials, or any other consumable asset. |

More Dangerous

| Complication | Description |
| :-- | :-- | 
| Breakage | A tool or other equipment you use in action is broken. |
| Defeat | You are defeated and probably grievously injured or near death. |
| Enimity | You antagonize someone or something present, possibly creating new or increased hostilities. |
| Inaccuracy | Your actions affect one or more persons or things you did not intend to affect. |
| Mishap | Unpredictable or unusual setbacks occur. These effects might be lingering or momentary.
| Peril | A new threat enters the scene, or an existing one becomes more hazardous. |
| Relocation | You end up somewhere different than you intended, which could be a problem.
| Snares | You become entangled in something you must deal with, and your options are limited until you do. |

### Ongoing Dangers

| Complication | Description |
| :-- | :-- | 
| Affliction | You are poisoned, on fire, diseased, or otherwise afflicted with something that causes ongoing harm. |
| Burdens | You are weighed down by your goods, troubles, or obligations and may suffer. |
| Ensorcelment | You are subjected to some controlling or transformative effect. |
| Fear | You suffer intense fear and may struggle with even usual activities. |
| Helplessness | You are caught or held and require assistance getting loose or free. |
| Hunger | You suffer from deprivation, hunger, thirst, or other deep need that must be satisfied. |
| Injury | You significantly damage your body, mind, or reputation. |
| Weariness | You grow tired enough to require rest before taking other actions. |

## Examples

Here are several examples of assigning complications to spark your creativity.

### Swing from a Chandelier

**Character**: “I swing from a chandelier over the heads of the tavern brawl.”

**GM’s complications**:

- **Relocation**: “Sorry, you land amid the brawl.”
- **Forfeit**: “Aww, you didn’t notice that coin purse on the table.“
- **Weariness**: “That was tougher than you expected. Take a round to catch your breath.”

### Truce Negotiations

**Character**: “As an upstanding hero of this settlement, I’ll negotiate a peace treaty with the hostile neighboring village.”

**GM’s complications**:

- **Enmity**: “You can never show your face again in the neighboring village.”
- **Burdens**: “You have to duel their champion.”
- **Delay**: “The negotiations take several days, and you miss your ship to Swordfish Island.”

### Fae Maze

**Character**: “Okay, we can find a way out. We hug the left wall until we find an exit.”

**GM’s complications**:

- **Expense**: “You use up a lot of rations as you travel.”
- **Snares**: “Brambles grow into and through your pack and clothes. You hear something big coming your way.”
- **Ensorcelment**: “That rose thorn pricked you, and you begin to... *Change*.”
- **Fear**: “You exit the maze after a long, bewildering slog. Add a *Frightened* constraint to your inventory—you will have nothing to do with the fae again.”


### Cliff Dive:

**Character**: “I leap from the castle wall onto the back of the beast and pierce its head with my spear.”

- **Failure**: “Your spear missed the monster.”
- **Defeat**: “The monster looks up and steps aside. You smash into the rocky ground and break bones. Make a *critical damage* save.”
- **Affliction**: “The horrible stench of the creature coats you and your gear.”

### Staff of Fireballs

**Character**: “I cast a firewall between us and the encroaching horde!”

**GM’s complications**:

- **Inaccuracy**: “You ignite parts of town around the horde.”
- **Peril**: “The wind blows the flame back towards you.”
- **Mishap**: “You smell smoke—your robes are on fire.”


### Gambit Examples

If your players use gambits in combat, you’ll often assign complications to attribute checks against monsters and opponents the party is battling.

Here are some ideas.

- **Failure**: Generally, failure means you don't affect or escape from the monster, though characters may have other goals.
- **Forfeit**: Some monsters provide extra opportunities to disarm, strip away their defenses, or trip them up in other ways. If forfeited, you miss out.
- **Breakage**: There are monsters with tough hides, ones whose presence rots leather or rusts away metal. Such monsters present this danger.
- **Peril**: Monsters that cause chaos or damage can include this trait; creatures with acid or magma for blood are especially likely to create perils if not fought with care.
- **Inaccuracy**: Some monsters move tactically, slipping between allies and around dangerous or valuable features or even teleporting away, so attacks can land badly, and combatants may end up hitting each other or things they care about.
- **Snares**: Attacks as simple as grabbing at a character can give this danger, but it's especially appropriate for constricting monsters or those with vines or tentacles under their control.
- **Enmity**: Some monsters focus on one target at a time or reserve their unique abilities for those that anger them most; such creatures will often have this danger. Other monsters can call upon or summon allies, presenting this danger differently.
- **Relocation**: Monsters that fling their foes around with brute physical force, gusts of breath, weather control, or magic all present this danger.
- **Defeat**: There are monsters whose merest touch is grievous harm. This is a fairly serious thing to throw around in most gameplay scenarios, but it can be appropriate.
- **Injury**: The majority of monsters have some ability to bite, claw, stab, or otherwise injure characters.
- **Affliction**: Venom, fire breath, and zombie plague attacks are all monstrous attacks that can create afflictions.
- **Burdens**: Any ability that drains vitality might be a good candidate for this danger.
- **Fear**: Some monsters are deadly in large part because their victims often panic while encountering them; if a monster is horrible enough, this might be a good danger.
